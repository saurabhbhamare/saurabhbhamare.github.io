Understanding Vectors for Game Development

Math plays a pivotal role in game development, especially when it comes to handling vectors. Let's delve into some essential concepts related to vectors that are crucial for game development.

Numbers and Their Positions

Numbers can be visualized as positions along a line, including both positive and negative values. This understanding is fundamental to grasping how vectors operate in a 2D space.

Distance Between Numbers

The distance between two numbers can be found using the absolute value of their difference: abs(B - A). This ensures a non-negative result.

Understanding Vectors

Vectors as Points in 2D Space

Vectors are pairs of numbers representing positions in a 2D coordinate system with x and y axes. These vectors can be visualized as arrows or points.

Vector Addition and Subtraction

Addition: Vectors are added by summing their corresponding components.

Subtraction: Vector subtraction is equivalent to adding the negated version of a vector. A - B is described as "the vector from B to A."

Distance Between Vectors

The Euclidean distance (straight-line distance) is calculated using the Pythagorean theorem: sqrt(x² + y²). The distance between two vectors is found by taking the magnitude of their difference: | B - A |.

Unit Vectors and Normalization

Unit Vectors: These are vectors with a magnitude of 1.

Normalization: This process scales a vector to have a magnitude of 1, calculated by dividing the vector by its magnitude: A / |A|.

Dot Product

The dot product of two vectors A · B is defined as (A.x * B.x) + (A.y * B.y). It's a single number that provides valuable information about the vectors' orientations:

Sign and Perpendicularity: A positive dot product means vectors point in roughly the same direction, a negative product means they are pointing opposite, and a zero dot product indicates perpendicularity.

Similarity: If both vectors are normalized, the dot product indicates "similarity."

Practical Game Development Examples

Car AI

Using Vectors for Car Movement and Steering:

Position and Direction Vectors: The position of the car and the direction it's facing can be represented by vectors. The direction vector can be used to determine where the car will move next.

Targeting and Navigation: By calculating the vector from the car's position to a target position (e.g., a waypoint or another car), the AI can determine the direction it needs to move. This is achieved using the difference vector: targetPosition - carPosition.

Avoidance: To avoid obstacles, the AI can use vectors to calculate potential collisions and adjust the car's path. The dot product can help determine if another object is in the car's path.

Example Code:


Vector2 carPosition = new Vector2(10, 5);
Vector2 targetPosition = new Vector2(20, 15);
Vector2 direction = (targetPosition - carPosition).normalized;

// Move car towards target
carPosition += direction * speed * Time.deltaTime;

// Avoidance example
Vector2 obstaclePosition = new Vector2(15, 10);
Vector2 toObstacle = obstaclePosition - carPosition;
if (Vector2.Dot(direction, toObstacle.normalized) > 0.8f) {
    // Adjust direction to avoid obstacle
    direction = Quaternion.Euler(0, 0, 45) * direction;
}
